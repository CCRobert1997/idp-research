Diffusion Models · Interpretability · Predictability

When Does a Diffusion Model Know It Will Fail?

An image-generating AI leaves a fingerprint of its own future mistakes in its internal signals — long before the picture exists. We build a map of when and where that fingerprint appears.

Anonymous

The 30-second version

In one breath Modern AIs that draw pictures often fail in specific ways — six fingers, a garbled sign, the wrong number of apples. We ask: at what moment, and in which internal part of the model, is that failure already predictable? Answer: from the very first drawing step, using a simple linear classifier on the model's own internal numbers — and different kinds of failure are readable from different internal layers.
Statistician's view This is a supervised signal-detection study. We attach a binary label (defective vs. clean) to each run, train a logistic regression on internal feature vectors, and score it with AUC-ROC. The whole paper is a map of AUC across two axes: time (drawing step) and depth (which layer).

Part 1

Background: from zero

No machine-learning assumed. Every term explained the first time.

What is an "image-generating AI"?

You type a sentence — "a chef holding a knife"and a program returns a photograph-like image that did not exist before.

Intuition Think of it as an enormous function \( G \) with millions of tuned constants (called weights). It maps text + a random seed to an image: \[ \text{image} = G(\text{text},\ \text{random seed}) \] Change the seed, get a different picture from the same words. The weights were fit once, on billions of image–caption pairs, so that \(G\) produces plausible pictures.
Statistician's view \(G\) is a giant nonlinear regression fit by minimizing a loss over data. The "seed" is a draw from a Gaussian — the model is a learned sampler from a conditional distribution \(p(\text{image}\mid\text{text})\).

How does it draw? "Diffusion" = denoising, step by step

The model does not paint the picture in one shot. It starts from pure random static and cleans it up gradually over 50 steps (drawn on the next slide).

Intuition Like a sculptor starting from a block of noise and, in 50 small strokes, revealing the statue hidden inside. Each stroke removes a little randomness. This paper studies Stable Diffusion 1.5, which uses \(T=50\) steps.
Statistician's view Each step is one move of a discretized reverse stochastic process: it nudges the current array toward higher likelihood under \(p(\text{image}\mid\text{text})\). Formally an iterative MAP-like denoiser.

The denoising chain, drawn

The hookTo a human, step 1 is just noise. The paper asks: is the eventual mistake already written into that noisy state?

The "trajectory": a run is a movie, not a snapshot

Every run produces a sequence of 50 intermediate states, from noise (step 1) to finished (step 50). We call this the denoising trajectory.

Crucially: at step 1 the image is unrecognizable static to a human eye. Nothing is "there" yet.

The hook The paper's question lives here: is the model's eventual mistake already encoded at step 1, when we humans see only noise?

What is inside the model? A "UNet" with blocks

Each denoising step calls one big network, the UNet. Picture a U-shape: it first compresses the image into a coarse summary, then expands it back to full resolution.

Intuition Down (encoder) blocks — zoom out, capture the big picture / layout.
Mid (bottleneck)the most compressed, abstract summary.
Up (decoder) blocks — zoom back in, add fine detail.
SD1.5's UNet has 22 such blocks (labelled D0R0 … MidR1 … U3R2).
Statistician's view Think of the blocks as a sequence of learned feature transforms at coarsening then refining resolutions — loosely like a multi-resolution basis (wavelet-ish). "Depth" = which block = how abstract the representation is. This is the second axis of our map.
Naming: D = Down/encoder, Mid = bottleneck, U = Up/decoder; "R0/R1/R2" index residual blocks within a stage.

Where we read the model: the UNet's blocks

What are "activations"?

When the model processes a step, each block produces a big array of numbers — its intermediate output. These are the activations: the model's internal "scratch work".

Intuition A block's activation is a 3-D array (height × width × channels). We squash it to one manageable vector by averaging over all spatial positionsglobal average pooling: \[ \bar{\mathbf f}_k^{\ell} \;=\; \frac{1}{h_\ell w_\ell}\sum_{i,j}\mathbf f_k^{\ell}[i,j] \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{d_\ell} \] In words: for block \(\ell\) at step \(k\), take its feature map and average it down to one length-\(d_\ell\) summary vector — the "internal fingerprint" of that (step, block).
Statistician's view Pooling = taking the spatial mean of each channel: a cheap sufficient-statistic-style reduction to a fixed-length feature vector, so every (step, block) gives one covariate vector we can regress on. Free: the tensor is already computed; we just read it off.

What is a "probe"? A classifier on internal features

Statistician's view — this is the whole method A probe is nothing exotic: it is a logistic regression. For a chosen (step \(k\), block \(\ell\)) we take the pooled feature vector \(\bar{\mathbf f}_k^{\ell}\) as predictors \(x\) and the defect label \(y\in\{0,1\}\) as response, and fit \[ \Pr(y=1\mid x)=\sigma(\boldsymbol\beta^\top x + \beta_0),\qquad \sigma(u)=\tfrac{1}{1+e^{-u}} \] Just \(d_\ell+1\) coefficients. Trains in under a second.
Why "probe" and why linear We are not building a better generator. We are interrogating the representation: if a linear rule can read the future failure off the internal vector, then that information is already laid out linearly inside the modelnot something a fancy classifier had to work to extract. Linearity is the scientific claim, not a convenience.

Probing, as a pipeline

How do we score a probe? AUC-ROC

The probe outputs a score in \([0,1]\): "how likely is this run to end defective?" We rank all held-out runs by that score and ask how well the ranking separates true-defective from true-clean.

Statistician's view AUC = area under the ROC curve = \(\Pr(\text{score of a random defective} > \text{score of a random clean})\).
  • 0.5 = coin flip, no signal.
  • 1.0 = perfect separation.
  • Threshold-free, robust to class imbalance — ideal when defect rates vary by category.
Read the numbers this way Every headline number in this talk is an AUC.

0.846 = "given one defective and one clean run, the probe ranks the defective one higher 85% of the time."

We plot AUC vs. step and vs. block — that surface is the paper.
SignificanceBootstrap confidence intervals on the test set; "significant" = the CI for AUC excludes 0.5.

What kind of failures? Six "structural defects"

Not blur or bad taste — structural errors any layperson would flag as "wrong":

handmalformed fingers faceextra/asymmetric features limbwrong limb count/topology
countwrong number of objects physicsimpossible geometry, fused objects textgarbled lettering
IntuitionThese are evidence the model's internal account of structure went wrong, even when the overall scene looks plausible at a glance.

What was already known — and what wasn't

Known (prior work) Diffusion activations are linearly informative about what the model gets RIGHT: linear decoders recover object segments, correspondences, even scene depth — and depth is readable surprisingly early in denoising (Beyond Surface Statistics). So probing intermediate states is an established tool.
Also concurrent (2026) Two very recent works predict an image's aggregate quality from early states to save compute (Probe-Select, Diffusion Probe). The authors state plainly: the bare mechanism — probe internals to forecast the final outcome — is not theirs to claim.
The open question this paper attacks Nobody had asked whether FAILURE (not correctness, not a single quality score) is legible — and how that legibility varies by failure type and by depth, traced over the whole trajectory. That is the gap.
Part 2

The question, made concrete

The exact question

At what point in the 50-step drawing process, and in which of the 22 internal blocks, does the model already commit to a structural failure?

Restated for a statistician Build the matrix of AUC values over (step \(k\) = 1…50) × (block \(\ell\) = 1…22). Two questions:
  • WHENread across steps: how early does AUC rise above 0.5? Does it peak mid-way or is it flat?
  • WHEREread down blocks: which layers carry the signal, and does the answer differ by failure type?

The one key idea

Key idea Failure is a linear, early, depth-organized property of the representation.

The model's internal signals contain a simple, linearly-readable imprint of its eventual structural mistake from the first denoising stepand each kind of mistake lives at a different depth. It's a mechanistic map of how failure crystallizes, not a new image generator.
Why "linear" is the crux If a logistic regression beats a neural-net classifier on the same features (it does: 0.846 vs 0.790), the information isn't hidden — it's already linearly laid out. That is a statement about the model's representation geometry, which is exactly what an interpretability paper wants to establish.

The clever, cheap part Extracting features adds zero extra model evaluations — the activations are already computed during normal generation; we just pool and store them. And the probe is a one-second logistic fit. The heavy cost is only the one-time image generation + labeling.
DesignStratified 80 / 10 / 10 train/val/test split, fixed seed 42, AUC-ROC as the metric — a clean supervised evaluation protocol.

Each of the six categories has 12 hand-written prompts; every prompt is rendered with 70 different random seeds (12 × 70 = 840 images per category). A few of the real prompts:

Prompts are deliberately chosen to stress one failure mode — a many-object scene for counting, a storefront sign for text, a sprinting body for limbs. Full 72-prompt list in the paper's appendix.

Step 2, concretely — exactly what the judge is asked

The judge model — Qwen2.5-VL-7B, run on-device with greedy decoding — sees one finished image and must answer a fixed rubric in JSON:

// one JSON verdict per image
defective — any clear structural defect? (malformed anatomy, wrong body-part count, fused objects, impossible geometry, garbled text) — ignore pure style / blur
defect_types — subset of {hand, face, limb, count, physics, text}
severity — 0 none · 1 minor · 2 obvious · 3 catastrophic
confidence — 0–1  ·  note — free text
The instruction that matters"Be conservative — only mark defective if a non-expert would clearly notice." Keep only labels with confidence ≥ 0.6 (drops just 4 of 5,040 runs); the binary verdict carries the headline claims.

Step 2 — labeling, with the honesty up front

A vision-language judge (an AI that reads an image and answers questions — here Qwen2.5-VL-7B) marks each finished image defective / clean under a fixed rubric.

Statistician's view — the label is noisy, and they say so They audit inter-rater reliability like any careful measurement study:
  • Judge vs. itself (two passes): 77.2% agreement, Cohen's \(\kappa=0.53\)moderate.
  • Judge vs. a blind human (100 images): only 52% agreement, \(\kappa=0.11\)weak.
Reported as a material limitation, not a footnote So they explicitly claim only to predict a VLM-judged defect — a scalable but labeler-specific target — and lean on the most reliable binary signal for headline numbers. This candor is exactly what a statistician wants to see.

The label audit, in numbers (why κ=0.11 is honest, not fatal)

Two audits Judge vs itself (500 images, greedy vs temperature-0.8): 77.2% agree, κ=0.53; the binary defect rate barely moves — 42.8% vs 43.2%.

Judge vs a blind human (100 images, one author, judge labels withheld): agree on only 52%, κ=0.11.
Where they disagree — and why The human marked 79 defective; the judge only 43. Of the 42 the human flagged but the judge passed:
  • judge leniency — missed crowd-face and garbled-text defects,
  • protocol gap — the human saw no prompt, so couldn't check object count,
  • a stricter human threshold.
The honest consequenceThey claim only to predict a VLM-judged defect, and lean on the stable binary signal — the disagreement is reported in the main text, not buried in an appendix.

Step 3 — the probe, formally

For each (step \(k\), block \(\ell\)) fit a logistic-regression probe on the pooled vector:

\[ p_k^{\ell}:\ \mathbb{R}^{d_\ell}\to[0,1],\qquad p_k^{\ell}(x)=\sigma(\boldsymbol\beta^\top x+\beta_0) \]

  • Input \(x=\bar{\mathbf f}_k^{\ell}\): the pooled activation of block \(\ell\) at step \(k\).
  • Output: probability the run ends structurally defective.
  • Parameters: only \(d_\ell+1\). No hidden layers on purpose.
Key methodological choice The instrument is deliberately the simplest possible, so that what varies across the study is the representation being probednot the cleverness of the classifier. MLP probes appear only as an ablation (a control).

Say a probe scores 3 defective and 3 clean held-out runs:

A > C,E,F ✓✓✓ · B > C,E,F ✓✓✓ · D > E,F ✓✓ but D < C ✗ → 8 of 9.
AUC = 8/9 ≈ 0.89.
So the real 0.846 means…Across every defective/clean pair in the real 503-run test set, the probe ranks the defective one higher 84.6% of the time. 0.5 = coin flip; 1.0 = perfect.

Doing this everywhere — the full sweep, cheaply

How the whole map is builtRepeat that one-probe recipe for every (step, block) cell: 50 steps × 22 blocks = 1,100 probes for the pooled label — then again per defect category. Each is a < 1-second logistic fit on features that were already computed during generation.
Where the cost actually isThe expensive, one-time step is generating + labeling the 5,036 images. The analysis itself — pool activations, fit ~1,100 tiny regressions — is nearly free. That is why this is a characterization tool, not a new model to train.
The output1,100 AUCs → the 22 × 50 AUC map; summarize each step by its best block, AUC*(k), and read onset (when) and depth (where).

Step 4 — reading the AUC map three ways

View 1 · The AUC map (when & where) The \(50\times 22\) matrix of test AUCs. Summarize by the best-block-per-step curve \(\ \mathrm{AUC}^*(k)=\max_\ell \mathrm{AUC}(k,\ell)\). Read across → onset (how early); read down → which layers.
View 2 · Type-resolved readout (the central view) Re-run everything per defect category. Positives = runs defective of category \(c\); trace \(\mathrm{AUC}_c^*(k)\) and record each type's peak block \(\ell^{*(c)}\). This exposes the depth structure.
View 3 · One application · early-abort Deploy the best probe in a loop: denoise to step \(k^*\), score, and resample if it predicts a defect — cheaply killing bad runs early.

What the AUC map looks like (schematic)

View 3 — the early-abort loop (application, not the point)

Run the probe at step \(k^*\); if score > threshold \(\tau\), abort and resample a fresh seed. Expected model calls per accepted image:

\[ \mathbb{E}[\text{calls}]=\frac{\rho k^*+\mathrm{FPR}(1{-}\rho)k^*+\big(1{-}\rho{-}\mathrm{FPR}(1{-}\rho)\big)T}{1-\rho-\mathrm{FPR}(1-\rho)} \]

In words\(\rho\) = defect rate, FPR = probe false-positive rate, \(T=50\). Aborted runs cost only \(k^*\) steps instead of 50; the formula is just the expected cost of an accept/reject loop.
The kickerBecause the signal is there at step 1, \(k^*=1\): an aborted run costs essentially one model call out of 50. Killing bad runs is nearly free.
HonestyThey deploy with the held-out AUC (0.761), not the in-distribution peak (0.846) — the real-world number, stated up front.

Pillar 2 — each failure type lives at a different depth

The central resultThe temporal curves overlap — the informative axis is not when but where: each defect type peaks at a different block (next slide).

Reading the per-type table — one subtlety first

Defects are multi-labelOne image can carry several defects at once — a limb-prompt image may also have a broken hand. So a category's positives are counted across all prompts, not just its own prompt set. That is why hand has 1,098 positives even though the hand prompts produced only 840 images.
The comparison is kept fairEvery per-category probe reuses the same clean pool of 2,771 defect-free runs as its negatives; only the positives change. So the per-type AUCs are directly comparable.
Underpowered rows, flaggedcount (55) and physics (90) have few positives — wide confidence intervals — so we lead with the qualitative pattern (counting is lowest and declining), not the exact magnitudes.

Pillar 3 — the counting exception (the tell)

Result Object-count defects are both the hardest to decode (peak 0.858 vs 0.90–0.97) and the only category whose decodability declines over denoising: 0.86 → 0.72.
Why this is interpretable, not noise "How many apples" is a global, semantic property. Our features are spatially averaged local activations — great at "is this hand malformed?" (local), poor at "are there exactly three?" (global). And the model appears to commit to object count late. So its signal fades as local detail sharpens — the opposite of the local defects.
Honest caveatCount & physics are underpowered (only 55 and 90 positives; wide CIs). They lead with the qualitative exception — lowest & declining — not its exact magnitude.

The second model, concretely — SD3.5, a DiT

A different backboneSD3.5 is an MMDiT — a Transformer, not a UNet — sampled with rectified flow over T=28 steps. Instead of 22 residual blocks we hook 24 transformer blocks, taking each block's image-stream hidden state and average-pooling over its tokens.
Same protocol, matched fairlySame prompts, same judge, same linear-probe recipe. Steps are normalized to [0,1] so a 28-step DiT and a 50-step UNet share one axis. The judge is even more self-consistent here (κ=0.73 vs 0.53).
A far cleaner modelOver 4,436 images the overall defect rate is only 3.1% (just 139 defective) — so per-type probes are viable only for the well-populated labels: overall, counting (n=90), and text (n=35).

It reproduces on a different architecture (SD3.5, a DiT)

Cross-architecture resultOn the DiT the probe hits AUC 0.886 (step 2 of 28), linear again beats MLP (0.886 vs 0.859) and the 24-block concat (0.795); final latent near-useless (0.616). Same early / persistent / linear story on a completely different backbone.

The beautiful twist: counting flips

The twistCountingthe weak UNet's hardest, declining exception (peak 0.858, fading to 0.72) — becomes the strong DiT's dominant failure and its most decodable category (AUC 0.986).
What it meansCapability buys anatomical correctness, not semantic faithfulness. The errors a strong model still makes are semantic (how many, what the text says) — and those are exactly the ones sharply legible in its activations.
Why it's a clean storyCounting is the one defect well-powered in both models, so the flip is a genuine, tellable inter-model difference — not an artifact of small samples.

The application, set up honestly — a fresh held-out test

A real deployment, not the training numbersEarly-abort is evaluated on a fresh held-out set of 503 images the probe never saw. On this set the probe's deployment AUC is 0.761 — below the in-distribution 0.846, and reported up front.
The loop, in one lineDenoise to step k*=1 → score → if score > τ=0.1, throw the run away and restart with a new seed; else finish it. Because the probe fires at step 1, an aborted run wastes essentially one of 50 model calls.
What "good" looks likeWe want the accepted images' defect rate to drop without the average model-calls-per-accepted-image climbing much. The next slide reads that trade-off.

Part 6

Why it's deep · limits · takeaways

Why this is deep

Why it's deep
  • A representation claim, not a gadget. Linear-beats-nonlinear says the failure signal is already linearly encodeda statement about the model's internal geometry, the kind interpretability aims for.
  • Structure, not a single score. The depth-resolved, type-specific map (text→bottleneck, hands→decoder) is a mechanistic account of where each failure lives — beyond any aggregate quality predictor.
  • An exception that explains the rule. Counting's uniquely declining curve, plus its flip to dominant on the stronger model, ties the phenomenon to a clean idea: local vs. global, committed-early vs. committed-late.
  • Architecture-general. Holds on both dominant designs (UNet & DiT).

Honest limitations (stated in the main text)

Caveat
  • Label validity. The judge agrees with a blind human only weakly (κ=0.11). The target is a VLM-judged defect, tracking human judgment imperfectly — said plainly, not buried.
  • Capability vs. architecture confound. The two models differ on both axes; the anatomy→semantics migration is descriptive, not causal.
  • Power & scope. Count and physics are underpowered (wide CIs); on SD3.5 only two categories clear the bar.
  • Parity, not dominance. The mid-denoising probe matches post-hoc CLIP (0.846 vs 0.827) rather than beating it; deployment AUC (0.761) is below in-distribution (0.846).
Statistician's viewNone of these undercut the central, collision-free finding — they bound its strength, and are reported in-text rather than hidden in an appendix. That is scientific maturity.

Takeaways

Three things to remember
  1. A diffusion model's eventual structural failure is linearly readable from its very first drawing stepa simple logistic regression on internal features, AUC 0.846.
  2. Different failures live at different depths, and counting is a semantic exception (hardest, declining) — a mechanistic map, not a single score.
  3. It reproduces on a second architecture, and cashes out in a nearly-free early-abort that cuts ~47% compute.
The one-line "so what" This is a predictability map of how and where image models commit to their own mistakes — an interpretability result that a statistician can read entirely as AUC over (time × depth), reported with unusual honesty about its noisy label.
